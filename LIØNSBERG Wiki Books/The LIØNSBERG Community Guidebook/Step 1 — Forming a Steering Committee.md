# Step 1 — Forming a Steering Committee

*Every body begins with a few sovereign cells that agree to become one.*

> **The work of this step:** gather the small founding circle that will hold your Community Company into being, give it a clear and temporary mandate, and hold its first real meeting. By the end you will have a named founding body, a draft purpose, and a date for the next gathering.

---

## Why a Steering Committee First

A community of 10,000 cannot found anything. Ten thousand people are a population, not a body. *Someone* has to begin — to hold the space, ask the right questions, and carry the work and the flame until the wider community becomes self-governing.

That someone cannot be a single person. Nor can it be a hundred people. Every great movement begins with a committed first few, who coalesce a strong working circle. Usually four to seven people is optimal. 

The **Steering Committee** is the founding Circle of your Community Company. It exists to do one thing: **bring the Community Company into being and then hand authority to it.** It is scaffolding, not the building. The day the wider community body can govern itself, the Steering Committee's founding mandate is complete — its members remain in the company, but their *temporary founding authority* dissolves back into the body. This should be named out loud from the first meeting; a founding body that forgets it is temporary becomes the ossified centralized node the Pattern exists to prevent.

## Who Belongs in the Founding Circle

As a rule of thumb, healthy founding circles are usually **4 to 7 committed people** who are **trustworthy, present, and willing to do the unglamorous founding work before surrendering authority.**  I

Some of the archetypical roles that often emerge include: 

- **A Driver** — one who feels the urgency and will not let the work die. 
- **A Weaver** — knows the community, knows who knows whom, can open doors and connect relationships. 
- **A Keeper** — reliable with information, details, money, dates, follow-through. 
- **An Elder** — trusted voice with standing in the community; lends legitimacy and transforms conflict. 
- **A Builder** — gets concrete things done in the physical and legal world.
- **A Questioner** — the one willing to say "I don't understand" and "are we sure?" Protects the body from groupthink. 

**Two cautions, learned the hard way:**

- **Do not recruit for prestige.** A big name who seldom attends  dramatically weakens the body. Celebrate the committed unknowns who show up every week and do the work. 
- **Diversity is strength.** A core function of the founding circle is to ask who is missing and consciously reach across the lines that have divided the community in the past — that reach is the first act of Unity.

## What Authority the Steering Committee Holds

Be explicit, in writing, from day one. The Steering Committee has authority to:

- convene meetings and invite people;
- draft (not ratify) the Community Company's Driver, Field of Agreements, and Charter;
- hold early shared resources in trust, transparently and accountably;
- speak *for the founding effort* — not *for the community*, which has not yet consented or expressed its shared voice.  

Every founding decision is provisional until the wider body ratifies it in Steps 3–6.

This is the living seed of the whole governance Pattern: 

- **a bounded [[Domains of Responsibility and Authority|Domain of Responsibility and Authority]], 
- a [[Selectively Permeable Membrane]] around membership, 
- a [[Field of Agreements]] everyone voluntarily consents to, 
- and a small [[Commons]] that is tended together.  

Good governance is practiced from the very first step and smallest scale, where mistakes are less costly and the integrity of the system is established. 

## The First Meeting

Ninety minutes. Use the **[[Steering Committee Starter Kit]]** for the full agenda, roles, and checklist. The shape:

1. **Open with the moment of silence and a question** (10 min). Read aloud, together: *"What is the One Thing that, if accomplished, would enable everything else to succeed?"* Let it land before anyone problem-solves. You are setting altitude, not making decisions.
2. **Name why each of you is here** (15 min). One at a time, uninterrupted. This is the first deposit in the [[Commons]] of trust.
3. **Draft a one-sentence purpose** (25 min). Fill in: _"The ___ Community Company exists for the total integrated wellbeing, development, right relationship, and flourishing of all generations of life in ___ ."_ Name your place. This is the stake in the ground, and the seed of your **Driver** — the one sentence everything else will hang from. Deepen it later with the **[[Driver Statement Worksheet]]**. The rest of the details will emerge. 
4. **Agree how you will work** (20 min). Five questions, written down: 
	1. What will we call ourselves?  
	2. How often do we meet? (Weekly)
	3. How do we decide? (Proposals and Consent, not majority — see the [[Consent Governance One-Pager]].) 
	4. What do we each commit to?  
	5. Who keeps the records?  
	6. How do we handle members joining or leaving? 

	This is your initial [[Field of Agreements]] — a single page, signed by the founding members. 
5. **Assign the next actions and set the next meeting date** (15 min). The three most important things, each with a name and a date. Do not leave without them. This is the key to sustaining momentum.  
6. **Close.** However your community closes a gathering with meaning.

## What "Success" Looks Like

You have completed Step 1 when you can point to:

- [ ] A **named founding circle** of 4–7 people who have each said yes out loud.
- [ ] A **one-sentence draft purpose** naming your place.
- [ ] A **one-page Field of Agreements** for how the Steering Committee itself will work, signed by all.
- [ ] A **written statement** of the Committee's authority *and its limits*, including that it is temporary.
- [ ] A **date and convener** for the next gathering.
- [ ] An **commitment log** with names and dates assigned to the three most important action items. 

If you have accomplished these six, you are becoming a body. The Community Company has begun.

> **A caution.** Most founding efforts fail not for lack of vision but for lack of *sustained effort.* The single highest-leverage act in this entire guidebook is showing up every week together at the time and place you set. 

## A Founding Rhythm — Provisional Now, Ratified After a Year

Do not try to write a perfect, permanent constitution in your first season. You cannot, and the attempt will paralyze you. Instead, follow the rhythm that wise foundings have always followed: **adopt your Charter *provisionally* now, live under it for a full year — four seasons — and *ratify* it only after you have felt where it holds and where it chafes.** Anchor the moments to the turning of the year if it helps: a soft launch, a first gathering to adopt the provisional Charter, a year of living and deliberation, and a second gathering to ratify what a year has taught you. A constitution earned through a year of practice is worth more than a flawless one written in a weekend. Everything is built to evolve, improve, and transform over time.
