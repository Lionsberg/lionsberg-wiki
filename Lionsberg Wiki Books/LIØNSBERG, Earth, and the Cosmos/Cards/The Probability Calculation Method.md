# The Probability Calculation Method

A 10-step transferable protocol for any family to compute their own collapse probability.

The urgency conversation is often blocked at the same point: _"Maybe you're right. Maybe the convergence is real. But maybe it isn't. I can't tell. So I'll wait until it becomes clearer."_ The waiting becomes permanent. The moment of action is deferred until the catastrophe has arrived — at which point action is too late.

The Probability Calculation Method ends this deferral by **handing the Pilgrim a tool to do the math themselves**. No belief required. No expert authority required. The Pilgrim sits at a kitchen table, runs the protocol, and emerges with their own numerical estimate of civilizational collapse probability over their own chosen timeframe. **Do not believe the Movement; do the math.**

The protocol has ten steps:

**1. Identify Factors.** List the systemic risk factors active in your time and place. The book's starting list runs to twenty (financial system fragility, ecological collapse indicators, technological-misalignment risk, geopolitical instability, supply-chain fragility, civilizational-cohesion indicators, etc.). Add your own. The list is yours.

**2. Assign Probabilities.** For each factor, assign a probability that it will resolve **benignly** over your chosen timeframe (the next 5 years, the next 10 years, the next 20 years). Be honest. _What is the realistic chance, in your judgment, that this factor returns to historical baseline rather than worsening or breaking?_

**3. Weight by Severity.** Not all factors carry equal weight. A breakdown in global food supply weighs more than a fashion-cycle disruption. Assign each factor a severity weight. The weighting is yours; the discipline is to justify the weighting.

**4. Calculate the Joint Probability.** Multiply the probabilities (adjusted for severity). With even a generous 50% chance of each of 20 factors resolving benignly, the joint probability that **all** resolve benignly is 0.5^20 = roughly 0.0001%. The math reveals the floor of "business as usual."

**5. Adjust for Interdependencies.** Real-world risks are correlated. Food failure cascades into political instability. Financial collapse cascades into supply-chain failure. Adjust the calculation: factors don't fail independently; they fail together. The joint probability of broad collapse rises sharply once interdependence is accounted for.

**6. Timeframe Adjust.** The further out, the higher the cumulative probability that some catastrophic factor materializes. A 5-year horizon has lower aggregated risk than a 20-year horizon. Compute your number for each horizon you care about.

**7. Confidence Intervals.** Your estimates are themselves uncertain. Add confidence ranges (±5%, ±10%). The discipline of stating your own uncertainty is itself the practice that distinguishes serious risk thinking from doomism or denial. Honest uncertainty is more useful than false precision.

**8. Scenario Plan.** Take the [[The Five Collapse Scenarios|Five Collapse Scenarios]] (Localized Crises ~20%, Widespread Social Unrest ~30%, Global Humanitarian Crisis ~25%, Near-Total Collapse ~15%, Extinction-Level Event ~10%) and weight them according to your own probability estimates. Where does your reasoned weight land? Different from the book's? That's fine — but commit to your numbers.

**9. Decide.** Now ask: _Given my numbers, what is the rational response?_ If your aggregate collapse probability over 10 years is 50%+, your behavior should reflect that. If it is 5%, your behavior should also reflect that. **The discipline is matching your behavior to your own numbers.** No more excuse of "I don't know what to think." You did the math. You think what the math says.

**10. Update.** Reality keeps producing evidence. Update your numbers as you encounter new information. Maintain a living document — your own ongoing assessment, revisited quarterly or annually. The Pilgrim who runs this protocol consistently over years develops calibrated risk-judgment that no consultant or pundit can match.

What the protocol does:

- **It transfers epistemic authority to the Pilgrim.** No longer waiting for experts to issue verdicts; the Pilgrim runs the analysis.
- **It removes the "I don't believe in collapse" objection.** The protocol doesn't ask the Pilgrim to believe anything; it asks them to make their own numerical estimates.
- **It produces calibrated action.** A Pilgrim with their own collapse probability number can rationally allocate resources, time, and effort.
- **It surfaces hidden assumptions.** As the Pilgrim assigns probabilities and weights, the assumptions underneath their worldview become visible — and either get defended or revised.
- **It is teachable.** A Pilgrim who has run the protocol can teach others. The conversation no longer requires "trust me"; it now offers "run the math."

The protocol also pairs with [[The Asymmetry of Consequences]]. Once the Pilgrim has their own probability estimate, they apply the matrix: even at low probability of collapse, the asymmetry between cost-of-action and cost-of-inaction tilts strongly toward mobilization. The combination — own-the-math + understand-the-asymmetry — produces the most rigorous case for the [[The Voluntary Leap Forward|Voluntary Leap Forward]] available in the entire LIØNSBERG corpus.

For the deeply analytical skeptic, the Pilgrim, this is the path. Skeptics often resist prophetic urgency. They rarely resist their own honest math. **Hand them the protocol. Let the math do the work.**
