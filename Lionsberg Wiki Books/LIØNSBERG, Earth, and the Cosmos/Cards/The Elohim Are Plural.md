# The Elohim Are Plural

The single most consequential mistranslation in human history.

Throughout the Hebrew Bible, the word **Elohim** (אֱלֹהִים) is **grammatically plural**. It takes plural verb-endings. It functions as a multiplicity. The Hebrew language has a singular form for "powerful one above" — **El** (אֵל) — and a slightly different singular variant — **Eloah** (אֱלוֹהַּ). Elohim is the plural. _Always._

English Bibles paste the singular word **"God"** over **Elohim** thousands of times throughout the Hebrew text. This single translation choice — repeated thousands of times across two millennia of translation — has produced the deepest distortion of humanity's actual Source Narrative. **A multiplicity of powerful ones from above** has been collapsed into a fictitious monotheistic singular, severing humanity from its actual cosmic history.

The mistranslation is not subtle:

- **Genesis 1:26** — _"And the Elohim said: Let **us** make humanity in **our** image, after **our** likeness."_ The plural Elohim speak in the first-person plural. The text is explicit. English translations soften this to _"And God said: Let us make..."_ — making the plurality invisible. The "Royal We" rationalization (Elohim is a plural-of-majesty for a singular God) is grammatically and contextually unsupported across the broader Hebrew corpus.
- **Genesis 3:22** — _"The Elohim said: Behold, the human has become like **one of us**."_ Again, plural Elohim, plural pronoun. The human is now "one of us" — explicitly a multiplicity of beings of whom one of us is a category.
- **Genesis 11:7** — at Babel — _"Come, let **us** go down and there confuse their language."_ Plural Elohim coordinating action.
- **Job 1:6** — _"The sons of the Elohim came to present themselves before Yahweh."_ The benei Elohim — the children of the Elohim — are real beings, distinct from Yahweh, distinct from each other.
- **Psalm 82:1** — _"Elohim takes his place in the divine council; in the midst of the Elohim he judges."_ The Hebrew explicitly has Elohim (singular use here) standing in the midst of Elohim (plural). The Divine Council is named. The plurality is operational.

The translation choice has theological-political function. The Roman Catholic Church and its predecessor consolidations in the late Second Temple period needed a **monotheistic** framework to legitimize a single political-religious hierarchy. A plural Elohim **threatens** that hierarchy. If the Source-Narrative speaks of many powerful ones, then the institutional claim to represent "the one true God" becomes precarious. The translation choice is **propaganda dressed as fidelity**.

The recovery of the plural Elohim opens the actual cosmic history the Bible has been carrying beneath the translation:

- **The Elohim are biological.** Genesis 6 ("the sons of the Elohim took wives from the daughters of humans, and they bore children to them") describes physiological reproductive compatibility between Elohim and humans. This is not metaphor. The Book of Enoch elaborates: the Watchers descended, taught arts, and bred the Nephilim (Giants). The Hebrew Bible witnesses to a populated cosmos of beings whose physiology overlaps with ours.
- **The Elohim are factional.** They are not a unified team. Some are aligned with [[ØNE]] / Elyon / the Most High; others are adversarial. Deuteronomy 32:8-9 (in the Dead Sea Scrolls reading): _"When the Most High [Elyon] apportioned the nations as inheritance, when he divided humanity, he established the boundaries of the peoples according to the number of the sons of Elohim. Yahweh's allotted portion was Israel."_ The text describes a **hierarchical allocation**: Elyon at the top, distributing nations among the Elohim, with [[Yahweh Is Not God|Yahweh]] receiving Israel as his specific portion.
- **The other Elohim are named in the text itself.** Chemosh of the Moabites. Astarte of the Phoenicians. Baal across Canaan. Moloch demanding child-sacrifice. Dagon of the Philistines. The Hebrew Bible repeatedly testifies that these are **real**, **territorial**, and **exercise demonstrable power**. They are not metaphors for nothing; they are other Elohim, allotted to other nations, operating within the same cosmic framework Yahweh operates in.
- **Yahweh is one Elohim among many.** Not the supreme Source. Not [[ØNE]]. Not Elyon. **Yahweh is a specific named Elohim** assigned to a specific tribal portion by the Most High. The conflation of Yahweh with the eternal Creator-Source is the foundational fraud of two empires of Western religion. (This claim is unveiled in detail in [[Yahweh Is Not God]] — companion Card.)

Once the plural Elohim are recognized, the rest of the Hebrew Bible reads differently. The "ye shall have no other gods before me" commandment is **not a denial that other gods exist**; it is Yahweh, a tribal Elohim, asserting his exclusive claim over Israel within a populated cosmic field. The conflict with the prophets of Baal is not Yahweh-versus-superstition; it is Yahweh-versus-rival-Elohim, demonstrably engaged across the same cosmic plane. The "wars in heaven" of Daniel and Revelation are intra-Elohim conflicts at a scale below the ONE.

This restoration of the plural Elohim **does not destroy the sacred**. The sacred remains. The Source remains. The deepest reality remains [[ØNE]]. What is destroyed is the **fraudulent collapse** of a populated cosmic field into a fictitious monotheistic singular — and the institutional power-claim that fraud has supported for two thousand years.

The Pilgrim doing serious study of the sacred texts adopts a simple discipline: **whenever reading "God" in English, mentally restore Elohim, El, Eloah, or Yahweh** depending on the underlying Hebrew. This single discipline opens the text to its actual cosmic richness. The reader who does this consistently within weeks notices that the entire shape of the Hebrew Bible **shifts** — it becomes a much more interesting, much more honest, much more cosmologically extensive text than the English translation permitted.

The Codex offers this restoration not to attack the Pilgrim's existing faith. The Codex offers this restoration to **deepen the Pilgrim's relationship with the actual Source** — beyond the institutional overlay that has obscured Him for two millennia.
